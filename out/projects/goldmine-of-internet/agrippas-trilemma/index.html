Agrippa's Trilemma

Agrippa's Trilemma is a problem in epistemology that questions how our beliefs are justified. It argues that any attempt to justify a belief ultimately leads to one of three unsatisfying options:

  1. Unsupported Beliefs: Our chain of justifications ends with a belief that is itself unsupported.
  2. Infinite Regression: Our chain of justifications goes on forever.
  3. Circular Reasoning: Our chain of justifications eventually circles back on itself.

The trilemma states that: Our beliefs are unsupported, or an infinite chain of justification supports our beliefs, or a circular chain of justification supports our beliefs.

Responses to the Trilemma

Epistemologists have proposed several responses to this problem. The main three are:

  • Foundationalism: This response accepts the first option, but argues that some beliefs (called "basic beliefs") are self-justifying and do not need further support. These foundational beliefs are the bedrock upon which all other knowledge is built.
  • Coherentism: This response accepts the third option, arguing that a belief is justified if it is part of a coherent system of beliefs. Justification is not a linear chain, but a holistic network.
  • Infinitism: This response accepts the second option, arguing that an infinite chain of justifications is not necessarily problematic.

Plausibility

Among these responses, Foundationalism is often considered the most plausible.
